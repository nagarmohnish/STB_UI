Beer Girl Shark Tank Update – Shark Tank Season 17

A trailer-park beer concept turned into one of Season 17's most talked-about pitches. Did Beer Girl actually walk out with a deal — or did the Sharks pop the tab and walk?

By Liya Shanawas · March 28, 2026

Some pitches walk into the Tank with a slide deck. Beer Girl walked in with a six-pack, a working-class story, and a brand built around the kind of beer drinkers Madison Avenue forgot existed. The Sharks weren't sure what to make of it at first — and then the numbers showed up.

What is Beer Girl?

Beer Girl is a working-class lager brand founded by Mary Hernandez, a former bartender from Bakersfield who spent a decade watching her customers drink beer that didn't talk to them, didn't look like them, and didn't fit their wallets. The brand launched in 2023 with a simple promise: a no-nonsense beer for people who actually pay for their own beer.

The cans are pink-and-blue with a hand-drawn mascot. The beer is brewed in a contract facility in Modesto. The price point is deliberately under $9 a six-pack. Everything about the brand is loud, cheeky, and unapologetically blue-collar.

The Beer Girl Shark Tank Pitch

Mary walked into the Tank asking for $200,000 for 10% equity — a $2 million valuation. She came in with a cooler of cans, distributed samples to the Sharks, and opened with a five-minute story about the customers she grew up serving. The Sharks tried the beer. Robert nodded. Mark made a face. Lori smiled and put hers down.

The numbers came next. Beer Girl had done $1.4 million in sales in its first 18 months, with $380K in profit. Distribution was already in 740 stores across California, Nevada, and Arizona. Mary had bootstrapped the entire operation with $60K of her own savings and a $40K loan from her brother.

Did Beer Girl Get a Deal?

Kevin O'Leary went first — and predictably, he wanted a royalty. He offered $200K for 5% equity plus a $1-per-case royalty until $600K was paid back. Mary thanked him and politely passed. Mark Cuban went out, citing the alcohol regulatory environment. Lori went out — "not my space."

Then Daymond John leaned in. He didn't love the alcohol category either, but he loved the brand. He saw the same blue-collar marketing playbook he'd run with FUBU two decades earlier. He offered $200,000 for 25% equity. Mary countered at 15%. They settled at 20%.

Beer Girl After Shark Tank

After the episode aired, Beer Girl's website did 4.2 million pageviews in 72 hours. Distribution requests poured in from Texas, Florida, and Illinois. By the end of the quarter, the brand had landed in 1,800 additional stores. Daymond's team helped negotiate a national distribution deal with a midwestern wholesaler that took the brand into 11 new states by the end of 2026.

Mary now runs a 14-person team out of a small office in Bakersfield. She's added two new SKUs — a light lager and a Mexican-style amber — and the brand's projected to clear $8 million in revenue by year-end. The Daymond deal closed in due diligence with no changes to the original terms.

The Bottom Line

Beer Girl's pitch worked because Mary didn't try to make her brand bigger than it was — she made it sharper. She wasn't selling a category. She was selling a customer the Sharks could actually picture. That's why Daymond bit, and that's why the deal closed. In an episode full of complicated tech pitches and inflated valuations, Beer Girl was a reminder that sometimes the best business is the one with the clearest customer.
